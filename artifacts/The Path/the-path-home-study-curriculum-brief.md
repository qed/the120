# The Path — Home Study Edition: Curriculum Brief

**Five phases. Twenty-five criteria. 125 unit tasks. All assessable at home, yes or no.**

This brief turns The Path (Sell → Build → Validate → Grow → Scale) into a home-study curriculum a parent-and-child team can run without the Saturday cohort. Every one of the 25 pass criteria from the 2026-27 program is broken into a step-by-step process of 4–6 unit tasks. Each task is binary — complete or not complete — with an evidence standard a parent can check without judgment calls.

## How to Use This Brief

**Roles.** The child is the founder. The parent is the coach and the verifier — never the doer. The parent's job on any task is to (a) keep it safe, (b) hold the evidence standard, and (c) resist fixing things. Where a task says "parent verifies," the parent checks the evidence against the *Done when* line and marks yes or no. There is no partial credit.

**Pacing.** Like the full program, the home edition is paced **by mastery, not calendar**. Tasks inside a criterion are sequential — complete them in order. Criteria inside a phase can run in parallel where life allows, but a phase is only passed when all five of its criteria are passed, and phases are completed in order. A fast 14-year-old might finish a phase in three weeks; an 8-year-old might take a term. Both are on The Path.

**The Evidence Folder.** On Day 1, create one folder (physical binder, shared drive, or both) called the child's **Founder File**. Every task's evidence — logs, one-pagers, photos, videos, screenshots, signed checklists — goes in it, labeled with the task ID. By the end of Phase 05 the Founder File *is* the completion portfolio. If evidence isn't in the file, the task isn't done.

**The assessment rule.** Every task ends with a ***Done when*** line. It is written to be answerable yes/no by a parent with no business background. If you find yourselves debating whether a task is done, the answer is no — do the visible thing the line asks for.

**The weekly rhythm (suggested).** Two or three working blocks per week plus one **Family Demo Session** every other Saturday. The Family Demo Session is the home-study stand-in for the cohort Saturday: a scheduled, slightly formal moment — phones down, an audience assembled — where presentations, pitches, and board meetings happen. Put it on the family calendar and treat it like a real event; the formality is doing pedagogical work.

## Home-Study Adaptations of Cohort Moments

Five criteria in the original Path reference the cohort, the Saturday workshops, or the intensives. The home edition replaces each with a **family-audience equivalent** that preserves the two things that matter: a real audience beyond the household's daily circle, and a real stage moment with stakes. The standing rule: **"non-family adult" means an adult who does not live in your home and is not a parent or guardian of the child** — a neighbor, a coach, a family friend, an aunt or uncle counts.

| Original criterion moment | Home-study equivalent |
|---|---|
| "Present the reasoning and outcome on a Saturday" (Validate) | Present at a scheduled Family Demo Session, calendared at least 3 days in advance |
| "Presented a 3-5 minute live demo" (Build) | Live demo at a Family Demo Session with at least one non-family adult present |
| "Present their financials to the cohort the way a founder presents to a board" (Grow) | A Family Board Meeting: parents plus at least one non-family adult sit as the board |
| "Pitch what the business becomes next year, on stage, at an intensive" (Scale) | The Capstone Showcase: an audience of at least five, including at least two non-family adults, with a designated "stage" moment |
| Demo Day Workshops (★ dates) | Fold into the Family Demo Sessions above; record every one on video for the Founder File |

## The Grade-Band Model

Every task carries three band variants, matching the program's book tracks: **Grades 3–5**, **Grades 6–8**, and **Grades 9–12**.

**What does not change across bands: the pass bars.** 25 outreach attempts, 40 customer contacts, 10 sales, four weeks of P&L — these numbers are the credential. A Grade 3 completion of The Path means the same thing as a Grade 12 completion.

**What changes across bands: autonomy, format, and depth.**

- **Grades 3–5 — Co-pilot.** The parent may scribe, dial, type, and drive the AI tools while the child directs every decision out loud. Written deliverables may be dictated, drawn, or built as posters. The child must be able to *explain* everything in the deliverable in their own words — that explanation is part of every verification.
- **Grades 6–8 — Support.** The child does the work; the parent reviews before anything is sent, published, or sold, and helps with account setup and logistics. Written deliverables are the child's own writing (typed or hand-written).
- **Grades 9–12 — Verify only.** The child works solo; the parent's role is safety supervision and yes/no verification. Most tasks add a stretch element — more polish, real tooling (spreadsheets, analytics), or a harder audience.

Where a band line is not listed for a task, the task is identical across bands.

**Safety rules (all bands, non-negotiable).** A parent is physically present for all in-person selling and outreach; a parent controls all accounts, payments, and publishing for children under 13 and reviews them for 13–17; no child's face, full name, school, or address in published content without explicit parent sign-off; all messaging to strangers goes through parent-approved channels.

## Task ID Scheme

Tasks are numbered `phase.criterion.task` — e.g., **2.3.4** is Phase 02 (Build), Criterion 3, Task 4. Criteria are numbered in the order they appear in the program's published pass criteria.

# Phase 01 · SELL — *Learn to confidently sell anything.*

> Entrepreneurship starts with a stranger saying yes. Before your child builds anything, they learn to sell something.

## Criterion 1.1 — Pitch a product in 60 seconds to an adult who isn't family, without notes

**1.1.1 — Pick the product and the one-liner.** Choose the product to pitch (something the child made or genuinely wants to sell) and write a single sentence: what it is, who it's for, why they'd want it.
*Done when:* the one-liner is written in the Founder File and the child can say it from memory.
- **3–5:** Parent scribes; child chooses the product and says the sentence unprompted.
- **6–8:** Child writes the sentence; parent may veto only on safety/feasibility.
- **9–12:** Child also writes one sentence on who the *wrong* customer is and why.

**1.1.2 — Write the full 60-second pitch.** Draft the pitch with four beats: hook, what it is, why it's good, and the ask. Maximum 150 words.
*Done when:* the written pitch is in the Founder File and reads aloud in under 60 seconds.
- **3–5:** Child dictates, parent types; child can point to each of the four beats.
- **6–8:** Child drafts alone; parent gives at most two notes.
- **9–12:** Child writes two versions with different hooks and picks one, noting why.

**1.1.3 — Rehearse to camera until note-free.** Practice on video until the child delivers the pitch without notes, under 60 seconds, three times in a row.
*Done when:* a video of three consecutive clean, note-free runs is in the Founder File.
- **3–5:** Runs may be up to 75 seconds; parent may hold up beat-reminder pictures (not words).
- **6–8:** As written.
- **9–12:** Third run must include a deliberate pause and eye contact with the lens — no racing.

**1.1.4 — Cold-pitch a parent and revise.** Pitch a parent who reacts as a real skeptical customer (one honest objection required). Child revises one thing in the pitch based on the objection.
*Done when:* the objection and the one revision are written under this task ID in the Founder File.
- **3–5:** Parent's objection is gentle but real ("Why would I need that?").
- **6–8:** As written.
- **9–12:** Two objections; the child must answer one live rather than revising for it.

**1.1.5 — Deliver to a non-family adult, no notes.** Pitch a non-family adult live (in person or video call), without notes, in under 60 seconds. The adult then says back what the product is and what was asked of them.
*Done when:* the adult's say-back matches the pitch (parent witnesses), and the date, adult's name, and outcome are logged. **This completes the criterion.**
- **3–5:** A familiar non-family adult (neighbor, coach) is fine.
- **6–8:** An adult the child doesn't see weekly.
- **9–12:** An adult the child has never pitched anything to, met for this purpose.

## Criterion 1.2 — Make a real sale: a real customer who isn't family, real money changing hands

**1.2.1 — Choose the offer and set the price.** Decide exactly what is being sold (product, service, or charity offer), what one unit is, and its price.
*Done when:* offer, unit, and price are written in the Founder File with one sentence on how the price was chosen.
- **3–5:** Parent lists three price options; child picks and says why.
- **6–8:** Child proposes the price; parent checks it covers costs.
- **9–12:** Price justified against two real alternatives a customer could buy instead.

**1.2.2 — Build the first prospect list.** With a parent, list ten real people or households (non-family) the child can safely ask, and how each will be reached.
*Done when:* a list of ten names/households with a channel for each is in the Founder File, parent-approved for safety.
- **3–5:** Drawn from the family's known circle (neighbors, teammates' families).
- **6–8:** At least three prospects outside the family's immediate circle.
- **9–12:** At least five outside the immediate circle, with a one-line reason each might buy.

**1.2.3 — Set up the point of sale.** Decide how money will actually change hands (cash box with float, parent-held e-transfer, square reader) and how the product will be delivered. Do one full dress rehearsal with a parent playing the buyer.
*Done when:* the rehearsal has run start to finish — greeting, ask, payment, delivery, thank-you — without stopping.
- **3–5:** Parent handles the money mechanics; child does everything else.
- **6–8:** Child handles money; parent watches the math.
- **9–12:** Child also prepares change/receipt handling and a simple sales record sheet.

**1.2.4 — Ask until one yes.** Work the prospect list, making real asks, until one real customer agrees and pays real money.
*Done when:* money from a non-family customer is in hand and the sale (who, what, amount, date) is logged.
- **3–5:** Parent physically present at every ask; child speaks the ask.
- **6–8:** Parent present but silent unless safety requires.
- **9–12:** Child runs the asks; parent verifies from the log afterward.

**1.2.5 — Deliver, thank, and log.** Deliver the product or service in full, thank the customer, and complete the sale record — including what the customer said.
*Done when:* the customer has what they paid for, and the completed sale record plus a photo (of the product, booth, or handoff — customer's face optional) is in the Founder File. **This completes the criterion.**
- All bands: as written; **9–12** adds one sentence on what they'd change about the sale process.

## Criterion 1.3 — Hear "no" at least three times and log what each no taught them

**1.3.1 — Build the No Log.** Create a log template with five fields: date, who was asked, exact words of the ask, what they said, what it taught.
*Done when:* the blank template is in the Founder File and the child can explain why collecting no's is the point, in their own words.
- **3–5:** Parent draws/prints the template; child decorates and explains it.
- **6–8/9–12:** Child makes it.

**1.3.2 — Collect and log No #1.** Make a real ask, hear a no, and fill in all five fields the same day. Talk it through with a parent: what did this no teach?
*Done when:* No #1 is fully logged, lesson included.
- **3–5:** Parent helps name the lesson; the words in the log are the child's.
- **6–8:** As written.
- **9–12:** Lesson must name something specific about the ask, not "try again."

**1.3.3 — Change one thing, then collect No #2.** Before the next round of asks, change exactly one thing (the offer, the opener, the audience, the price) and write it down. Then make asks until the next no, and log it.
*Done when:* the pre-declared change and the fully logged No #2 are both in the file.
- All bands: as written; **9–12** notes whether the change made the no *different* in kind.

**1.3.4 — Collect and log No #3.** Continue asking; log the third no with all five fields.
*Done when:* No #3 is fully logged.
- All bands: as written.

**1.3.5 — Write the three-lesson summary.** Summarize: the three lessons, one pattern across the no's, and one change now permanent in how the child sells.
*Done when:* the summary (5–8 sentences or a poster) is in the Founder File and the child presents it aloud to a parent. **This completes the criterion.**
- **3–5:** Poster or dictated page.
- **6–8:** Written page.
- **9–12:** Written page plus the ratio of asks to no's to yeses so far.

## Criterion 1.4 — Explain cost, price, and profit for a product created by them, on one page

**1.4.1 — Count every cost.** List every real cost of making and selling one unit — materials, packaging, fees, ingredients — with actual numbers from receipts or store prices.
*Done when:* the per-unit cost list is in the Founder File and totals correctly (parent checks the math).
- **3–5:** Parent gathers prices with the child; child does the adding (calculator fine).
- **6–8:** Child researches prices; parent spot-checks two.
- **9–12:** Includes at least one non-obvious cost (their time, transaction fees, spoilage/waste).

**1.4.2 — Justify the price.** Write the price and *why*: what two alternatives cost, and why a customer would pay this much for this product.
*Done when:* the price justification (3–5 sentences) naming two real alternatives is in the file.
- All bands: as written; **9–12** adds what the highest defensible price would be and why they didn't choose it.

**1.4.3 — Do the profit math.** Compute profit per unit (price − cost) and total profit so far from real sales.
*Done when:* both numbers are computed correctly in the file and the child answers two "what if" questions from the parent (e.g., "If the price drops a dollar, what happens to profit?") correctly, out loud.
- **3–5:** Whole-dollar rounding fine; parent asks one "what if."
- **6–8:** As written.
- **9–12:** Also computes profit margin as a percentage.

**1.4.4 — Build the one-pager.** Assemble cost, price, and profit onto a single page with at least one visual (drawing, diagram, or chart).
*Done when:* the one-pager exists, fits on one page, and contains all three numbers plus the visual.
- **3–5:** Poster format; parent may assemble, child directs layout.
- **6–8:** Child makes it, paper or digital.
- **9–12:** Digital, with a simple chart (drawn in a spreadsheet or tool).

**1.4.5 — Teach it back.** Present the one-pager to a parent playing a curious investor who asks three questions. The child answers all three without help.
*Done when:* the parent confirms all three answers were the child's own and correct. **This completes the criterion.**
- **3–5:** Questions are concrete ("Which costs the most to make?").
- **6–8:** One question is a "what if."
- **9–12:** One question is adversarial ("Why wouldn't I just buy the cheaper one?").

## Criterion 1.5 — Complete 25 supervised outreach attempts: a booth, door to door, calls, or messages

**1.5.1 — Choose channels and write the safety plan.** Pick at least two outreach channels (booth, door-to-door, calls, messages) and write the family safety plan: where, when, who supervises, what's off-limits.
*Done when:* channels and the signed safety plan (parent and child both sign) are in the Founder File.
- All bands: as written; channel choice should fit the child's age and neighborhood.

**1.5.2 — Script the openers and build the tracker.** Write a short opener for each channel and create a tracker numbered 1–25 with columns: date, channel, who, response, note.
*Done when:* openers are written and the blank 25-row tracker is ready.
- **3–5:** Parent builds tracker; child authors openers aloud.
- **6–8:** Child builds both.
- **9–12:** Tracker also captures a follow-up column.

**1.5.3 — Attempts 1–5, shoulder to shoulder.** Complete the first five attempts with the parent alongside, then debrief: what was scariest, what worked, what to change.
*Done when:* rows 1–5 are filled in and the debrief's one change is written down.
- **3–5:** Parent may open the conversation; child makes the ask.
- **6–8:** Child runs the whole attempt; parent within earshot.
- **9–12:** Parent observes only.

**1.5.4 — Attempts 6–15, applying the change.** Complete ten more attempts using the declared change. Review the tracker mid-way with a parent.
*Done when:* rows 6–15 are filled in and a one-line mid-point review note is added.
- All bands: supervision per the safety plan.

**1.5.5 — Attempts 16–25 and the funnel count.** Finish the final ten attempts, then compute the funnel: attempts → real conversations → yeses.
*Done when:* all 25 rows are complete and the three funnel numbers are written at the bottom of the tracker. **This completes the criterion.**
- **3–5:** Parent helps count; child states the numbers aloud.
- **6–8:** As written.
- **9–12:** Adds one sentence on which channel converted best and a guess why.

# Phase 02 · BUILD — *Make a real product with AI.*

> Your child stops being a user of technology and becomes a builder with it.

## Criterion 2.1 — Ship a working product, site, or offer built with AI tools, with a live URL, pricing, and instructions

**2.1.1 — Write the product spec.** Half a page: who it's for, what it does, what it will cost, and what "working" means for v1.
*Done when:* the spec is in the Founder File and the child can state the user and the job in one breath.
- **3–5:** Dictated to parent; child draws the product.
- **6–8:** Child writes it.
- **9–12:** Spec also lists what v1 deliberately *won't* do.

**2.1.2 — Set up the AI toolkit.** With a parent, choose the AI build tools and set up accounts (parent-owned per the safety rules). Do one 15-minute warm-up build together to learn the tool.
*Done when:* tools are chosen, accounts work, and the throwaway warm-up build exists.
- **3–5:** Parent drives the keyboard; child directs every prompt aloud.
- **6–8:** Child drives; parent reviews prompts before sending.
- **9–12:** Child sets up solo; parent verifies account safety settings.

**2.1.3 — Build v0.1.** Build a first rough version with the AI tools — enough that a parent can click it, use it, or hold it.
*Done when:* a parent has used v0.1 for two minutes without the child touching it, and a screenshot/photo is filed.
- **3–5:** Parent executes prompts the child composes; every design decision is the child's.
- **6–8:** As written.
- **9–12:** v0.1 includes a working core interaction, not just static pages.

**2.1.4 — Add pricing and instructions.** Put the price and plain-language "how to use this" instructions into the product itself.
*Done when:* a stranger opening the product would know what it costs and how to use it, verified by the parent reading it cold.
- All bands: as written; **9–12** instructions must survive the parent following them literally, step by step.

**2.1.5 — Go live and test from outside.** Publish to a live URL (or, for a physical offer, a public order page/flyer with a reachable contact). Test it from a different device on a different network.
*Done when:* the live URL loads on someone else's device, the link is logged in the Founder File, and a parent confirms pricing + instructions are visible. **This completes the criterion.**
- **3–5:** Parent publishes; child verifies on the second device.
- **6–8:** Child publishes with parent review.
- **9–12:** Child publishes solo; also asks one non-family person to open it and confirm.

## Criterion 2.2 — Explain in a 1-page brief how the product connects to a gap in a domain they know

**2.2.1 — Map what you know.** List three domains the child actually knows well (a sport, a game, a hobby, a subject, a community) and pick one.
*Done when:* the list of three and the pick, with one sentence on why, are in the Founder File.
- All bands: as written.

**2.2.2 — Hunt the gap.** Gather evidence of a real annoyance or unmet need in that domain: talk to three people who share it, or list five specific frustrations from the child's own experience.
*Done when:* the raw evidence (interview notes or the frustration list) is in the file.
- **3–5:** Parent sits in on interviews; child asks the questions.
- **6–8:** Child interviews; at least one interviewee outside the household.
- **9–12:** All three interviewees outside the household; notes capture quotes.

**2.2.3 — Name the gap in one sentence.** Compress the evidence into a single gap statement: "People who ___ struggle to ___ because ___."
*Done when:* the sentence is written and a parent can repeat the gap back accurately after hearing it once.
- All bands: as written.

**2.2.4 — Write the brief.** One page: the domain, the evidence, the gap statement, and exactly how the product fills the gap.
*Done when:* the brief fits one page and contains all four parts.
- **3–5:** Dictated/scribed; child's words.
- **6–8:** Child writes.
- **9–12:** Brief also names one competitor or existing workaround and why the product beats it.

**2.2.5 — The outside-reader test.** A non-family reader (adult or peer who knows the domain) reads the brief cold and says back the gap and the solution. Revise until their say-back is right.
*Done when:* the reader's accurate say-back is witnessed by a parent and the final brief is filed. **This completes the criterion.**
- All bands: as written; **9–12** reader should be someone from the domain itself.

## Criterion 2.3 — Contact 40 potential customers; launch one piece of marketing with metrics

**2.3.1 — Build the 40-contact list.** With a parent, assemble a list of 40 real potential customers (people, households, or community groups) with a channel for each. Booth passers-by and event attendees count when logged individually.
*Done when:* the 40-row list exists and the parent has approved every channel for safety.
- **3–5:** Parent sources most contacts; child sorts who's most likely to buy and why.
- **6–8:** Child builds at least half the list.
- **9–12:** Child builds the list; at least 10 contacts beyond the family's existing circle.

**2.3.2 — Write and approve the outreach message.** Draft the message or script, one per channel. Parent reviews and approves before anything is sent.
*Done when:* approved scripts are in the Founder File, marked APPROVED with the date.
- All bands: as written — the approval gate applies to every band.

**2.3.3 — Contact 1–20.** Make the first twenty contacts. Track every one: date, who, channel, response.
*Done when:* rows 1–20 are complete.
- **3–5:** Parent sends written messages the child composed; child handles in-person contacts.
- **6–8:** Child sends from parent-supervised accounts.
- **9–12:** Child runs it; parent audits the tracker weekly.

**2.3.4 — Contact 21–40 and tally.** Finish the list, then tally: contacted → replied → interested → bought.
*Done when:* all 40 rows are complete and the four-number tally is written at the bottom. 
- All bands: as written.

**2.3.5 — Design the marketing piece and pick its metric first.** Create one piece of marketing (flyer, poster, short video, social post) and — before launching — write down the one metric that will define "it worked" (scans, replies, visits, sales) and the number to beat.
*Done when:* the finished piece and its pre-declared metric + target are in the file.
- **3–5:** Child designs, parent produces; metric target set together.
- **6–8:** Child makes it; parent approves before it goes anywhere public.
- **9–12:** Piece includes a trackable mechanism (code, dedicated link, "mention this flyer").

**2.3.6 — Launch, measure, conclude.** Launch the piece, let it run for a set window (1–2 weeks), then read the metric and write three sentences: what happened vs the target, why, and what you'd change.
*Done when:* the metric reading and three-sentence conclusion are in the Founder File. **This completes the criterion.**
- All bands: as written; **9–12** compares the marketing channel's results to the direct-outreach results from 2.3.4.

## Criterion 2.4 — Ship a v2 that responds to feedback from at least three real users

**2.4.1 — Watch three real users.** Recruit three real users (non-family preferred; one family member allowed for 3–5) to try the product while the child watches silently and takes notes on where they hesitate, stumble, or quit.
*Done when:* three sets of observation notes are in the Founder File.
- **3–5:** Parent may take the notes the child dictates immediately after each session.
- **6–8:** Child takes notes live.
- **9–12:** Sessions include one "think aloud" user; notes capture direct quotes.

**2.4.2 — Turn notes into a ranked fix list.** Convert observations into a list of possible improvements, then rank and pick the top three.
*Done when:* the ranked list exists, each of the top three traceable to a specific user moment.
- All bands: as written.

**2.4.3 — Build the three changes.** Implement the top three changes using the AI tools, keeping a simple before/after change log.
*Done when:* all three changes work and the change log (what changed, which user prompted it) is filed.
- **3–5:** Parent drives the tools; child directs each change and confirms it matches the user note.
- **6–8:** Child builds with parent review.
- **9–12:** Child builds solo.

**2.4.4 — Ship v2 live.** Publish v2 to the live URL (or updated offer), replacing v1.
*Done when:* v2 is live and a parent confirms each of the three changes is visible/working in the shipped version.
- All bands: publishing rules per band as in 2.1.5.

**2.4.5 — Close the loop with the users.** Tell all three users what changed because of them. At least one tries v2 and confirms the change addressed their issue.
*Done when:* the thank-you messages and one user's confirmation are in the Founder File. **This completes the criterion.**
- All bands: as written; messages via parent-approved channels.

## Criterion 2.5 — Present a 3–5 minute live demo: the build, the results, and the lessons

**2.5.1 — Outline the demo.** Structure it in four parts: the gap (from 2.2), the live product walkthrough, the numbers (from 2.3), and three lessons about building.
*Done when:* the one-page outline is in the Founder File.
- All bands: as written; **3–5** may be a picture storyboard.

**2.5.2 — Rehearse against the clock.** Practice with the real, live product (not screenshots) until two consecutive runs land between 3 and 5 minutes.
*Done when:* two timed clean runs are logged (parent timestamps them).
- **3–5:** Parent may run the device while the child presents.
- **6–8:** Child runs their own demo.
- **9–12:** One rehearsal must survive a parent-injected surprise ("the wifi is slow — keep going").

**2.5.3 — Stage the Family Demo Session.** Calendar a Family Demo Session at least three days out and invite the audience: both parents/household adults if possible, plus **at least one non-family adult**.
*Done when:* the invitation is sent and the session is on the family calendar.
- **3–5/6–8:** One non-family adult.
- **9–12:** Two non-family adults, at least one with business or domain experience.

**2.5.4 — Deliver the demo and take questions.** Present live, product running, 3–5 minutes, then take at least two audience questions.
*Done when:* the demo ran live within time and both questions were answered by the child alone — parent verifies.
- All bands: as written.

**2.5.5 — Record and file.** Video the demo (or record the call) and file it with the outline and one sentence from the child: the single biggest lesson about building.
*Done when:* the video and lesson sentence are in the Founder File. **This completes the criterion.**
- All bands: as written.

# Phase 03 · VALIDATE — *Test ideas like a scientist.*

> Most business ideas are wrong. The skill is finding out fast and cheap.

## Criterion 3.1 — Run at least 2 validation loops: hypothesis, test, outcome

**3.1.1 — Build the loop template.** With a parent, create the validation loop template: hypothesis ("We believe ___"), test ("We will ___"), pass bar ("It's true if ___ happens by ___"), result, decision (persevere / pivot / kill).
*Done when:* the blank template is in the Founder File and the child explains, in their own words, why the pass bar is set *before* the test.
- **3–5:** Parent builds the template; the explanation is the child's.
- **6–8/9–12:** Child builds it.

**3.1.2 — Loop 1: write the bet before the test.** Fill in hypothesis, test design, and pass bar for a real question about the business (will people pay more? do customers want delivery? does the flyer work better than the post?). The test must be runnable within two weeks and cost under an agreed cap.
*Done when:* all three pre-test fields are complete and dated *before* any testing starts.
- **3–5:** Parent proposes two testable questions; child picks and fills the template aloud.
- **6–8:** Child writes the loop; parent checks the pass bar is measurable.
- **9–12:** Child writes it solo; the hypothesis must be one the child genuinely believes could fail.

**3.1.3 — Loop 1: run it and decide.** Run the test exactly as designed, record the result, compare it to the pre-set bar, and write the decision.
*Done when:* result and decision fields are complete, and the decision follows the bar (not the child's hopes) — parent verifies the logic.
- All bands: as written.

**3.1.4 — Loop 2: a second, different bet.** Write and pre-date a second full loop testing a *different kind* of question than Loop 1.
*Done when:* Loop 2's three pre-test fields are complete and dated before testing.
- All bands: as written; **9–12** must design a cheaper or faster test than Loop 1.

# Phase 04 · GROW — *Turn a validated idea into a running business.*

> One sale is a story. Repeat sales are a business.

## Criterion 4.1 — 10 sales or 3 repeat customers

**4.1.1 — Build the sales ledger.** Create the running ledger every sale will enter: date, customer, item, amount, new or repeat.
*Done when:* the ledger exists, back-filled with every sale to date, and the new/repeat column is accurate.
- **3–5:** Parent builds the ledger; child back-fills it.
- **6–8/9–12:** Child builds and maintains it.

**4.1.2 — Set the weekly selling routine.** Decide when selling happens every week — a standing slot ("Saturday morning booth," "Wednesday order messages") — and put it on the family calendar.
*Done when:* the routine is calendared and has run for its first week.
- All bands: as written; the routine, not the mood, drives the asks.

**4.1.3 — Run the repeat campaign.** Go back to every past customer with a reason to buy again: a new item, a refill offer, a loyalty deal. Contact each one.
*Done when:* every past customer has been re-contacted (logged), whatever they answer.
- **3–5:** Parent sends messages the child composes; in-person re-asks are the child's.
- **6–8:** Child contacts with parent review.
- **9–12:** Child designs the offer and runs the campaign solo.

**4.1.4 — The halfway review.** At 5 total sales or 1 repeat customer, sit down with a parent and answer three questions from the ledger: where do sales come from, what's the best seller, what will we double down on?
*Done when:* the three answers are written in the Founder File.
- All bands: as written.

**4.1.5 — Hit the bar.** Keep the routine running until the ledger shows **10 total sales or 3 repeat customers**.
*Done when:* the ledger verifiably shows the bar is met — parent audits every row against real money received. **This completes the criterion.**
- All bands: same bar. **9–12** adds: state the revenue total and the single best decision that drove it.

## Criterion 4.2 — Track a simple P&L for four consecutive weeks of active business

**4.2.1 — Build the P&L template and learn its lines.** Create a weekly P&L: money in (sales), money out (costs), profit. The child explains each line to a parent in their own words.
*Done when:* the blank four-week template exists and the explanation is given.
- **3–5:** Three lines only, whole dollars; parent builds, child explains.
- **6–8:** Child builds it on paper or spreadsheet.
- **9–12:** Spreadsheet with formulas; adds a cumulative profit row.

**4.2.2 — Set up the records habit.** Create one place receipts and sales records land every time (envelope, photo folder, note) so the P&L is filled from records, not memory.
*Done when:* the system exists and the first week's records are in it.
- All bands: as written.

**4.2.3 — Weeks 1–2.** Fill in the P&L for two consecutive weeks of active business, from records.
*Done when:* two weeks are complete and every number traces to a record — parent spot-checks two entries.
- All bands: as written.

**4.2.4 — Weeks 3–4.** Complete weeks three and four consecutively — no gaps. If a week has no business activity, the clock restarts.
*Done when:* four consecutive weeks are filled in from records.
- All bands: as written.

**4.2.5 — The month review.** Circle the best week and answer in writing: why was it best, and what one change will next month test?
*Done when:* the review is written and the child presents the four-week P&L to a parent, walking through every number without help. **This completes the criterion.**
- **3–5:** Presentation verbal, review dictated.
- **6–8:** As written.
- **9–12:** Adds week-over-week profit trend and one sentence on what the trend means.

## Criterion 4.3 — Create one daily or weekly repeating AI process that supports the business

**4.3.1 — Pick the chore worth automating.** List the repeating chores in the business (reminders, social drafts, inventory counts, thank-you messages, bookkeeping) and pick one that happens at least weekly. Write what it takes to do by hand.
*Done when:* the chosen chore and its by-hand description (steps + minutes) are in the Founder File.
- All bands: as written.

**4.3.2 — Design the AI process.** Using Claude Cowork or another AI agent/tool, write the instructions or prompt that will do the chore on a schedule. The child authors the instructions; the words must be theirs.
*Done when:* the written process instructions are filed and set up in the tool.
- **3–5:** Parent operates the tool; child dictates and refines the instructions.
- **6–8:** Child sets it up on a parent's account with review.
- **9–12:** Child sets it up solo.

**4.3.3 — Supervised first run.** Run the process once with the child watching the output closely. Compare it to the by-hand version; fix the instructions where it fell short.
*Done when:* the first run's output and at least one instruction fix are documented.
- All bands: as written.

**4.3.4 — Let it run on schedule.** Let the process run at least two more cycles on its own schedule (two days or two weeks, per its cadence).
*Done when:* the child shows a parent evidence of two unattended runs and what each produced.
- All bands: as written.

**4.3.5 — Write the process card.** One card/half-page: what the process does, when it runs, how to check it worked, how to stop it. Filed so anyone could take it over.
*Done when:* the process card is in the Founder File and a parent can answer "how would I check it ran this week?" from the card alone. **This completes the criterion.**
- **3–5:** Dictated to parent.
- **6–8/9–12:** Child writes it.

## Criterion 4.4 — Close at least one real negotiation with documented terms

**4.4.1 — Learn the moves.** With a parent, learn four negotiation basics — the ask, the counter, the trade, the walk-away point — and role-play a scenario twice, once as each side.
*Done when:* both role-plays have run and the child can name all four moves with an example.
- **3–5:** Scenarios are concrete and playful (negotiating the price of a hot chocolate stand's supplies).
- **6–8:** As written.
- **9–12:** Adds anchoring and silence as moves; role-play includes a hostile counterpart.

**4.4.2 — Pick the real negotiation and set the sheet.** Choose a real counterparty and stake — supplier price, bulk discount, booth fee, commission split, trade of services — and privately write the goal, the opening ask, and the walk-away point *before* the conversation.
*Done when:* the pre-negotiation sheet is dated and filed before contact.
- **3–5:** Counterparty may be a known adult (store owner, market organizer); parent arranges the meeting, child negotiates.
- **6–8:** Child arranges and negotiates; parent present.
- **9–12:** Child arranges and negotiates; parent may be out of the room where safe.

**4.4.3 — Negotiate.** Have the real conversation. The child speaks for themselves; the parent does not step in on substance.
*Done when:* the negotiation has concluded — deal or no deal — and the child writes what happened, including which moves they used.
- All bands: as written. A dignified no-deal above the walk-away line counts as a completed negotiation *conversation* — but the criterion needs a closed deal, so if no deal, pick a new counterparty and repeat 4.4.2–4.4.3.

**4.4.4 — Document the terms and confirm both sides.** Write the agreed terms — who does what, for how much, by when — and have both parties confirm (signature, reply, or witnessed handshake note).
*Done when:* the terms document with both confirmations is in the Founder File.
- **3–5:** Parent formats the document; terms are the child's words.
- **6–8:** As written.
- **9–12:** Adds a comparison: final terms vs the pre-negotiation sheet — what was won, what was given.

**4.4.5 — Deliver the deal.** Perform the child's side of the terms in full, on time, and get the counterparty to confirm they got what was agreed.
*Done when:* delivery evidence and the counterparty's confirmation are in the Founder File. **This completes the criterion.**
- All bands: as written — a negotiation is only closed when both sides have what they shook on.

## Criterion 4.5 — Present their financials the way a founder presents to a board

*Home-study note: "the cohort" becomes the Family Board Meeting — parents plus at least one non-family adult, sitting as a board.*

**4.5.1 — Build the board pack.** Three to five slides or pages: sales to date, costs, profit trend across the P&L month, and what's next.
*Done when:* the board pack exists and every number in it traces to the ledger or P&L.
- **3–5:** Child chooses what goes on each page; parent assembles; numbers are the child's.
- **6–8:** Child builds it.
- **9–12:** Includes one chart and one honest "what's not working" slide.

**4.5.2 — Own the numbers.** Rehearse until the child can answer "why?" for every number without reading — why was week 3 best, why is that cost so high.
*Done when:* a parent quizzes any three numbers from the pack and the child answers all three cold.
- All bands: as written.

**4.5.3 — Convene the board.** Schedule the Family Board Meeting; recruit at least one non-family adult to sit on the board with a brief to ask real questions, not cheerlead.
*Done when:* the meeting is calendared and the board (parents + non-family adult) is confirmed.
- **3–5/6–8:** One non-family adult.
- **9–12:** Two non-family adults; at least one who reads financial statements in real life.

**4.5.4 — Present and take the hard questions.** Deliver the board pack and take at least three questions, at least one of which is uncomfortable. No parent rescues.
*Done when:* the meeting has run, recorded on video, with all questions answered by the child.
- All bands: as written.

**4.5.5 — Write the board memo.** Afterward, the child writes what the board pushed on, what they conceded, and one commitment for next month — and sends it to the board members.
*Done when:* the memo is in the Founder File and sent. **This completes the criterion.**
- **3–5:** Dictated; parent sends.
- **6–8:** Child writes; parent sends.
- **9–12:** Child writes and sends.

# Phase 05 · SCALE — *Build systems so the business runs beyond them.*

> The final step is the founder's step: making the business bigger than one person's hours.

## Criterion 5.1 — Automate one real part of the business with an AI agent or automation, and show it running

*Note: this goes further than Grow's repeating process (4.3) — this automation must run a real business function end to end without the child in the loop. It may be a major upgrade of the 4.3 process or something new.*

**5.1.1 — Map the work and pick the target.** Map everything the business does weekly, mark what's already automated, and pick one real function (order intake, scheduling, customer replies, restock alerts, invoicing) to fully automate.
*Done when:* the work map and the chosen target, with a sentence on why this one, are in the Founder File.
- All bands: as written.

**5.1.2 — Baseline the by-hand cost.** Measure how long the function takes by hand across one normal week (minutes, and steps).
*Done when:* the baseline number is written down with how it was measured.
- **3–5:** Parent times; child counts steps.
- **6–8/9–12:** Child measures.

**5.1.3 — Build the automation.** Build it with an AI agent or automation tool. The child authors the logic — what triggers it, what it does, what "done" looks like.
*Done when:* the automation exists and completes the function once with the child initiating but not intervening.
- **3–5:** Parent operates the tool; every rule and decision is dictated by the child.
- **6–8:** Child builds with parent review.
- **9–12:** Child builds solo, with error handling for one thing that could go wrong.

**5.1.4 — The hands-off test.** Let the automation handle the function for one full real cycle (a real order, a real week of replies) with the child not touching it.
*Done when:* evidence of the untouched cycle — logs, outputs, timestamps — is in the Founder File.
- All bands: as written.

**5.1.5 — Demo it live with the savings math.** Show a parent the automation running end to end, live, and present the math: minutes per week by hand vs now, and what the child does with the recovered time.
*Done when:* the live demo has run and the savings math is filed. **This completes the criterion.**
- All bands: as written; **9–12** also names the automation's weakest point and the watch-out.

## Criterion 5.2 — Delegate a task with written instructions that worked

**5.2.1 — Choose the task and the delegate.** Pick a real recurring business task and a delegate: a friend, a sibling, or an AI agent. The task must matter — if it's skipped, a customer would notice.
*Done when:* task and delegate are named in the Founder File, with the "a customer would notice" test answered.
- **3–5:** Sibling or parent-as-employee is fine; AI agent with help.
- **6–8:** Friend, sibling, or AI agent.
- **9–12:** Prefer a human delegate — instructions for humans are the harder skill.

**5.2.2 — Write instructions that need no questions.** Write the instruction sheet: steps in order, what "done" looks like, an example of good output, and what to do if stuck. Standard: the delegate should need zero clarifying questions.
*Done when:* the instruction sheet is complete with all four parts.
- **3–5:** Child dictates; parent scribes verbatim, including the unclear bits — they're the lesson.
- **6–8:** Child writes.
- **9–12:** Child writes; includes a quality checklist the delegate self-checks against.

**5.2.3 — Hand off and stand back.** Give the delegate the sheet and let them do the task. The child answers no questions — questions get written down instead, as instruction bugs.
*Done when:* the delegate has attempted the task using only the sheet, and every question they asked is logged as a bug.
- All bands: as written.

**5.2.4 — Grade the output and fix the instructions.** Compare the delegate's output to the written "done" standard. Fix every instruction bug, then have the delegate run it once more.
*Done when:* the second run meets the written standard with zero questions — that is the "instructions that worked" bar.
- All bands: as written; repeat the fix-and-rerun loop until the bar is met.

**5.2.5 — File the final playcard.** File the final working instruction sheet with a note: what had to change between v1 and the version that worked.
*Done when:* the final sheet and the what-changed note are in the Founder File. **This completes the criterion.**
- All bands: as written.

## Criterion 5.3 — Keep the business serving customers through a week they took off

**5.3.1 — Plan the off week.** Pick the actual week (calendar it) and list everything the business must still do that week: orders, deliveries, replies, posts.
*Done when:* the week is calendared and the must-still-happen list is complete.
- All bands: as written.

**5.3.2 — Cover every item.** Assign every list item to a system: the automation (5.1), the delegate (5.2), pre-work done in advance, or — where truly necessary — an honest customer notice ("orders ship again Monday"). No item may be assigned to "the child will quickly check."
*Done when:* the coverage plan shows every item covered and a parent has challenged it ("what happens if an order comes in Tuesday?") with answers from the plan.
- **3–5:** Parent may be one of the assigned systems (as a briefed employee with written instructions, not as a rescuer).
- **6–8:** As written.
- **9–12:** No parent coverage; automation, delegate, and pre-work only.

**5.3.3 — Dry-run one day.** Before the off week, run one full day hands-off as a rehearsal. Fix what broke.
*Done when:* the dry-run day has run, and fixes (if any) are logged.
- All bands: as written.

**5.3.4 — Take the week off.** The child does no business work for the full week. The parent verifies daily — the discipline of *not* working is the task.
*Done when:* the week has passed with zero child interventions (parent attests), and at least one customer was actually served during the week — an order, a delivery, a reply — with evidence.
- All bands: as written. If no customer interaction naturally occurred, extend until one does; a week of silence proves nothing.

**5.3.5 — The re-entry review.** Back from the week: what held, what broke, what the fix is. Written and reviewed with a parent.
*Done when:* the review is in the Founder File with at least one system improvement adopted. **This completes the criterion.**
- All bands: as written.

## Criterion 5.4 — Write the one-page playbook someone else could use to run it

**5.4.1 — Outline the machine.** List every recurring thing the business needs done — daily, weekly, monthly — in one skeleton outline.
*Done when:* the outline covers the full business (parent challenges: "what about X?" until nothing's missing).
- All bands: as written.

**5.4.2 — Write the one-pager.** Compress the outline into one page: the weekly rhythm, the tools and where they live, how money is handled, who to contact when stuck. (A parent-held key list for logins may ride alongside, not on, the page.)
*Done when:* the playbook fits one page and covers every outline item.
- **3–5:** Dictated; may be a large illustrated page.
- **6–8:** Child writes.
- **9–12:** Child writes; page includes the three numbers to watch weekly.

**5.4.3 — The stranger test.** Hand the playbook to someone who has never run the business (the delegate from 5.2 doesn't count). Using only the page, they run one full day or cycle. The child watches silently, logging every stumble.
*Done when:* the test has run and every stumble is logged.
- **3–5:** Tester may be the other parent or an older sibling.
- **6–8:** Non-household tester preferred.
- **9–12:** Non-household tester required.

**5.4.4 — Fix and finalize.** Revise the playbook to remove every logged stumble; the tester (or a second tester) confirms the confusing parts now read clean.
*Done when:* the final playbook is in the Founder File with the tester's confirmation.
- All bands: as written.

**5.4.5 — Live by the page.** For one full week, the playbook is the business's source of truth: the child runs the week from the page, and anywhere reality and the page disagree, the page gets corrected the same day.
*Done when:* the week has run with the playbook as the working checklist, and the page either needed zero corrections or shows its dated corrections. **This completes the criterion.**
- All bands: as written — a playbook nobody runs from is a poster.

## Criterion 5.5 — Pitch what the business becomes next year, on stage

*Home-study note: "on stage, at an intensive" becomes the Capstone Showcase — a real staged moment at home or a booked space: an audience of at least five, including at least two non-family adults, a designated stage area, and a printed program if the family wants to go full theatre. This is the finale of The Path; make it feel like one.*

**5.5.1 — Draft the vision pitch.** Build the pitch: this year's real numbers (from the Founder File), what the business becomes next year, and what it will take to get there. Three slides or one poster, 3–5 minutes.
*Done when:* the draft pitch exists and every backward-looking number in it is real and traceable.
- **3–5:** Child directs; parent assembles visuals.
- **6–8:** Child builds it.
- **9–12:** Includes one concrete next-year target (revenue, customers, or product) and the first step toward it.

**5.5.2 — Rehearse to performance standard.** Practice until the pitch runs 3–5 minutes from memory, twice consecutively, including handling one planted audience question.
*Done when:* two clean timed runs with the planted question are logged.
- All bands: as written.

**5.5.3 — Produce the Showcase.** Plan the event like a founder: date at least one week out, invitations sent, venue arranged (living room transformed, community room, backyard), stage area set, recording arranged.
*Done when:* five-plus attendees including two non-family adults are confirmed, and the logistics checklist is complete.
- **3–5:** Parent produces; child hosts.
- **6–8:** Child produces with parent handling bookings.
- **9–12:** Child produces end to end.

**5.5.4 — Take the stage.** Deliver the pitch live at the Showcase, then take audience questions. This is a performance: introduced by name, applause allowed, no restarts.
*Done when:* the pitch was delivered live within time to the assembled audience, on video, with questions answered by the child alone.
- All bands: as written.

**5.5.5 — Seal the Founder File.** File the Showcase video, then complete the portfolio: every criterion's evidence checked present, the full checklist signed by parent and child, and one final page written by the child — "What I can do now that I couldn't do a year ago."
*Done when:* the signed checklist and final page are in the Founder File. **This completes the criterion — and The Path.**
- All bands: as written. The final page is the child's alone, every band.
